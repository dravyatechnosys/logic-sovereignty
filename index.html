ARCHITECTURAL PERSPECTIVE

22 Years of Observation

I started writing code in 2004 (C, C++, COBOL, VB.NET). Like every developer, I learned to translate business requirements into working code. For years, that felt right.

But around year — after building healthcare platforms, ERPs, government solutions, and financial applications — I noticed something uncomfortable: The technology kept changing. The problem did not.

Every system I worked on had business logic buried inside application code. Discount rules in controllers, approval workflows in functions, eligibility conditions scattered across services and databases. Nobody questioned it. But I kept asking:

If the business changes its policy tomorrow — why does a developer need to be involved?
